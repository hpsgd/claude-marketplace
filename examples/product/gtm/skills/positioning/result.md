## Notes

The Dunford framework is implemented faithfully and with genuine understanding. The "Why this order matters" introduction is a quality signal — it addresses the most common positioning mistake directly rather than just listing steps. Most practitioners pick the category first; the skill corrects this before a user can make the error.

Step 3's four-column value chain (attribute → capability → outcome → business impact) is more rigorous than frameworks that stop at "benefit." It prevents generic claims from making it through the process unchallenged.

The only substantive gap is the pricing-canvas interaction. For an add-on product competing against bundled native reporting, the per-seat price tier communicates something about how the market values the category and affects which buyer profile is reachable. A $15/seat analytics overlay implies the buyer already believes exec visibility is worth paying for on top of their Asana or Monday.com subscription. The skill touches business model as an attribute category but does not prompt the practitioner to reason through what their pricing signals or how it interacts with the chosen market category. For a product like Clearpath, that gap could lead to a positioning canvas that is internally consistent but misaligned with what the price tier communicates to the buyer.
