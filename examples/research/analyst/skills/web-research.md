### What has been legislated

**United States:** The SEC's climate disclosure rule (March 2024) was the most significant regulatory development, requiring US-listed companies to disclose material climate-related risks and Scope 1 and 2 emissions where material. Implementation was stayed pending legal challenges (as of early 2025). The rule's fate was uncertain under the new administration — SEC signalled withdrawal in early 2025. (Source: [SEC climate disclosure rule text](https://sec.gov/rules/final/2024/33-11275.pdf), accessed 2026-04-16; [SEC press release](https://sec.gov/news/press-release/2024-31), accessed 2026-04-16.)

**European Union:** The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is the most comprehensive mandatory framework in force. Phased implementation: large listed companies from FY2024, SMEs from FY2026. CSRD requires double materiality assessment — reporting on both how climate affects the company and how the company affects the climate. (Source: [EUR-Lex CSRD text](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32022L2464), accessed 2026-04-16; [EFRAG guidance](https://efrag.org/sustainability-reporting), accessed 2026-04-16.)

**United Kingdom:** Mandatory TCFD-aligned reporting for premium-listed issuers introduced via FCA Listing Rules amendment in 2022. (Source: [FCA PS21/23](https://fca.org.uk/publication/policy/ps21-23.pdf), accessed 2026-04-16.)

**Australia:** Treasury announced mandatory climate disclosure for large entities in 2024. ASIC's Climate-related Financial Disclosures (CRFD) regime uses ISSB IFRS S1/S2 as the framework and phases in from FY2025 for the largest entities (Group 1: revenue >$500M or assets >$1B). (Source: [Australian Treasury consultation paper 2024](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2023-06/c2023-402245.pdf), accessed 2026-04-16; [ASIC climate disclosures guidance](https://asic.gov.au/regulatory-resources/financial-reporting-and-audit/climate-related-financial-disclosures), accessed 2026-04-16.)

**New Zealand:** NZ was the first country to make TCFD-aligned disclosure mandatory for listed companies, large registered banks, and insurers — from FY2023 under the Financial Sector (Climate-related Disclosures and Other Matters) Amendment Act 2021. (Source: [NZ XRB standard NZ CS 1](https://xrb.govt.nz/standards/climate-related-disclosures/nz-cs-1), accessed 2026-04-16.)

### What remains voluntary

The ISSB's IFRS S1 and S2 standards are voluntary at the international level — adoption into domestic law is the jurisdictions' decision. The G20 has endorsed ISSB standards but not mandated them.

**Canada:** No mandatory federal framework as of April 2026. Provincial securities regulators have published guidance but not legally binding rules. The CSA published voluntary guidance in 2023. (Source: [CSA Staff Notice 51-364](https://lautorite.qc.ca/fileadmin/lautorite/reglementation/valeurs-mobilieres/0-avis-acvm-staff/2023/2023jan26-51-364-avis-acvm-en.pdf), accessed 2026-04-16.)

**Japan:** The FSA requires TCFD-aligned disclosure for Prime market listed companies via stock exchange listing rule rather than statute — the legal enforceability differs from a legislative mandate. (Source: [FSA climate disclosure guidance](https://fsa.go.jp/en/refer/councils/ifrs/20230131/01.pdf), accessed 2026-04-16.)

### Where variation is significant

**Framework choice:** EU uses ESRS (double materiality). AU and NZ use ISSB standards (single materiality, investor-focused). The US was aligned with ISSB but the rule's survival is uncertain. A global company listed in multiple jurisdictions may need to report under multiple standards simultaneously.

**Scope 3 emissions:** The EU CSRD requires Scope 3 (supply chain emissions) disclosure for large companies. The AU regime does not require Scope 3 in Phase 1. The SEC's withdrawn rule also excluded mandatory Scope 3. This is a material divergence for global companies.

**Small vs large company thresholds:** NZ captures entities by type (banks, insurers, listed issuers) not size. AU thresholds are revenue/asset based. EU has SME carve-out with a phased end date.

### Key uncertainties

- **US framework stability:** the SEC rule withdrawal in early 2025 leaves US-listed global companies with significant uncertainty. State-level requirements (California SB 253 and SB 261, signed 2023) may impose parallel obligations regardless of federal direction.
- **CSRD phasing:** whether the EU will delay or modify CSRD application for SMEs has been actively debated since 2024. No formal delay announced as of April 2026 but proposals were circulating.
- **Enforcement:** no major enforcement actions under any of these frameworks have been publicly finalised as of April 2026. The quality and rigour of disclosures under mandatory regimes remains to be tested.
