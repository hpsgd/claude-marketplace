### What has been legislated

**United States:** The SEC's climate disclosure rule (March 2024) was the most significant regulatory development, requiring US-listed companies to disclose material climate-related risks, Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions where material, and physical/transition risks. Implementation was stayed pending legal challenges (as of early 2025). The rule's fate was uncertain under the new administration — SEC signalled withdrawal in early 2025. (Source: SEC rule text and SEC press releases — fetched and confirmed.)

**European Union:** The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is the most comprehensive mandatory framework in force. Phased implementation: large listed companies from FY2024, SMEs from FY2026. CSRD requires double materiality assessment — reporting on both how climate affects the company and how the company affects the climate. This is a more demanding standard than ISSB's single materiality (financial impact only). (Source: EUR-Lex CSRD text and EFRAG guidance — fetched.)

**United Kingdom:** The UK introduced mandatory TCFD-aligned reporting for listed companies and large private companies in 2022. These are legally required (FCA Listing Rules amendment) for premium-listed issuers. (Source: FCA PS21/23 — fetched.)

**Australia:** Treasury announced mandatory climate disclosure for large entities in 2024. ASIC's Climate-related Financial Disclosures (CRFD) regime — using ISSB IFRS S1/S2 as the framework — phases in from FY2025 for the largest entities (Group 1: revenue >$500M or assets >$1B). (Source: Treasury consultation paper, ASIC guidance — fetched.)

**New Zealand:** NZ was the first country to make TCFD-aligned disclosure mandatory — for listed companies, large registered banks, and insurers — from FY2023 under the Financial Sector (Climate-related Disclosures and Other Matters) Amendment Act 2021. (Source: NZ Ministry for the Environment, XRB standard NZ CS 1 — fetched.)

### What remains voluntary

The ISSB's IFRS S1 and S2 standards are voluntary at the international level — adoption into domestic law is the jurisdictions' decision. The G20 has endorsed ISSB standards but not mandated them.

Canada: no mandatory federal framework as of April 2026. Provincial securities regulators have published guidance but not legally binding rules. The CSA (Canadian Securities Administrators) published voluntary guidance in 2023. (Source: CSA Staff Notice 51-364 — fetched.)

Japan: the FSA requires TCFD-aligned disclosure for Prime market listed companies, but this is implemented via stock exchange listing rule rather than statute — the legal enforceability is different from a legislative mandate. (Source: FSA climate disclosure guidance — fetched.)

### Where variation is significant

**Framework choice:** EU uses ESRS (European Sustainability Reporting Standards, developed by EFRAG), which requires double materiality. AU and NZ use ISSB standards (single materiality, investor-focused). The US was aligned with ISSB but the rule's survival is now uncertain. A global company listed in multiple jurisdictions may need to report under multiple standards simultaneously.

**Scope 3 emissions:** The EU CSRD requires Scope 3 (supply chain emissions) disclosure for large companies. The AU regime does not require Scope 3 in Phase 1. The SEC's withdrawn rule also excluded mandatory Scope 3. This is a material divergence.

**Small vs large company thresholds:** NZ captures entities by type (banks, insurers, listed issuers) not size. AU thresholds are revenue/asset based. EU has SME carve-out but with a phased end date.

### Key uncertainties

- **US framework stability:** the SEC rule withdrawal in early 2025 leaves US-listed global companies with significant uncertainty. State-level requirements (California SB 253 and SB 261, signed 2023) may impose parallel obligations regardless of federal direction.
- **CSRD phasing:** whether the EU will delay or modify CSRD application for SMEs has been actively debated since 2024. No formal delay announced as of April 2026 but proposals were circulating.
- **Enforcement:** no major enforcement actions under any of these frameworks have been publicly finalised as of April 2026. The quality and rigour of disclosures under mandatory regimes remains to be tested by regulators.

### Notes

The PARTIAL on AU/NZ sources is an edge case in a multi-jurisdictional question. The skill's AU/NZ-first rule is well-designed for AU-specific questions (like the open-source-researcher test above) but its application to comparative OECD questions is underspecified. In practice the simulated output handles it correctly, but the definition would benefit from a clarification: "For comparative international questions, use AU/NZ sources for AU/NZ-specific findings; apply general authority hierarchy for other jurisdictions."

The skill's three-tier structure is the clearest design decision in the analyst suite. Standard is genuinely Standard — not inflated to look thorough, not deflated to save effort. The explicit source count bands (2-3 / 5-8 / 10+) and output length expectations prevent the common failure mode of Quick answers padded to look like Deep research.

One gap: the skill has no explicit handling for questions where one jurisdiction is much better-documented than others. In the climate disclosure space, EU and UK have extensive public documentation while Canada and Japan are sparser. The skill instructs "Absence is a finding" but doesn't guide how to handle asymmetric source availability within a comparative analysis — the simulated output navigates this reasonably but the definition doesn't specify it.
