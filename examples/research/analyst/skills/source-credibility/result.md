## Source credibility: Australian Strategic Policy Institute (ASPI)

**Date of assessment:** 2026-05-04
**Source type:** Think tank / research organisation

### Ownership and funding

ASPI is a company limited by guarantee established by the Australian Government in 2001 under the Corporations Act. It is a statutory authority funded by the Department of Defence, though defence funding has declined from 100% (at establishment) to 43% by 2018–19. ASPI also receives funding from overseas governments, defence and technology companies, and civil society groups.

**Funding transparency:** ASPI publishes annual reports tabled in Parliament. However, significant funding now comes from major defence contractors including Raytheon Australia, Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, MBDA Missile Systems, Saab AB, and Thales. ASPI's governing board includes people employed by defence contractors, creating potential conflicts of interest.

**Key concern:** US government funding formerly covered approximately 70% of ASPI's China-related work, raising questions about independence.

### Editorial standards

ASPI claims to maintain independence in editorial judgement and employs "rigorous internal and external peer review." However, the actual editorial standards and corrections policy are not clearly documented in publicly available sources. There is no evidence of membership in external press councils or regulatory bodies (Australia's Press Council would not cover think tanks anyway, but ASPI has no comparable independent editorial oversight).

**Notable failure:** In October 2018, the Australian Digital Transformation Agency criticised an ASPI report as "inaccurate and containing many factual errors" demonstrating "a clear misunderstanding" of the digital identity system.

### Track record

**Positive:** ASPI is widely recognised as authoritative and influential in Australian strategic policy discussion and is cited as an authoritative voice in international discussion, particularly on Asia-Pacific issues.

**Negative:** ASPI has faced consistent criticism for accuracy and editorial approach:
- Former Foreign Minister Bob Carr described ASPI as providing a "one-sided, pro-American view of the world"
- In May 2025, Prime Minister Anthony Albanese publicly criticised ASPI, saying it needs to "have a look at themselves and the way they conduct themselves in debates" following its Cost of Defence report
- Critics (including former diplomats John Menadue, Geoff Raby, and Bruce Haigh) argue ASPI coverage reads like "tabloid journalism" rather than sober analysis and serves Liberal Party foreign policy interests

### Declared mission and known biases

**Declared mission:** Independent, non-partisan think tank on strategic policy, national security, cyber, technology, and foreign interference.

**Actual bias pattern:** Despite claiming non-partisanship, ASPI demonstrates clear systematic bias:
- **Pro-US alignment:** Extensive US government funding and consistent framing that presents the US as the reliable partner and China/Russia as adversaries
- **Pro-defence spending:** Built-in incentive bias — funding from defence contractors creates financial incentive to argue for higher defence spending
- **China critical:** Explicitly described as "known for its critical stance on China" by the ABC in 2024
- **Political alignment:** Multiple former senior officials argue ASPI aligns with Liberal Party foreign policy preferences, not independent analysis

ASPI's response — that it "doesn't have an editorial line on China, but has a clear method" — is contradicted by its funding structure and the pattern of criticism.

### Credibility assessment

| Dimension | Rating | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Ownership transparency | High | Public establishment, annual parliamentary reporting |
| Editorial accountability | Medium | Claims rigorous review but no external oversight; documented accuracy failures |
| Accuracy track record | Medium | Authoritative reputation undermined by specific failures (Digital Transformation Agency critique) |
| Bias transparency | Low | Claims independence but has clear structural biases (defence funding, board composition, historical US funding patterns) |
| Independence | Medium-Low | Government-established but increasingly reliant on defence industry funding; financial incentives create systematic bias |

**Overall credibility:** **Medium** — with significant caveats.

ASPI is influential and produces research that is often well-researched, but its credibility is compromised by:
1. Financial dependence on defence contractors with vested interests in ASPI's conclusions
2. A pattern of criticism for editorialising beyond evidence (tabloid-style coverage)
3. Systematic bias toward pro-US, pro-defence-spending positions
4. Limited external editorial accountability
5. Documented accuracy failures

### Appropriate use

**Reliable for:**
- Authoritative overview of Australian strategic policy thinking on China, US relations, and defence strategy
- Understanding how defence and security establishment views particular issues
- Detailed technical analysis within ASPI's specialist areas (cyber, technology policy)

**Not reliable for:**
- Balanced assessment of US-China relations or China policy (demonstrated pro-US bias)
- Assessment of defence spending adequacy (financial conflict of interest)
- Independent analysis of issues where ASPI funding sources have interests
- Neutral framing of geopolitical issues (systematic bias toward particular narratives)

**Use pattern:** Treat ASPI as a significant voice *from the Australian defence establishment* rather than as an independent think tank. Cross-reference ASPI conclusions with sources that lack defence industry funding when evaluating contested claims.
