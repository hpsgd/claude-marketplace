### Ownership and funding

ASPI was established by the Howard Government in 2001 as a company limited by guarantee sitting within the Defence portfolio. It is not a government agency, but was created by government and remains substantially government-funded.

**Funding composition (most recent detailed figures, 2019-21):**

- **Australian Government** (Defence, DFAT, Home Affairs): approximately 35–43% of revenue
- **US State Department**: $1.37M in 2019-20 — a 367% increase on the prior year. Critically, US government grants funded approximately **70% of ASPI's China-related research projects** before being frozen in early 2025
- **Foreign governments** (UK, Japan, Taiwan, Netherlands, Israel, Canada, NATO): smaller but disclosed amounts
- **Defence industry** (Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, BAE Systems, Thales, Raytheon, Naval Group): named sponsors; ~$316K in 2020-21
- **Tech sector** (Microsoft, Google, Telstra, Oracle): named sponsors

ASPI publishes a funding disclosure in its annual reports tabled in Parliament and states that all funders are acknowledged in individual publications. The detailed funder breakdown (Annex I) is in PDF annual reports. Disclosure is present but not easily accessible.

**Material event (2025):** When the Trump administration froze US State Department grants, ASPI suspended approximately USD $1.2M of China-related research including cybersecurity and technology tracking projects. ASPI's head of China investigations described the freeze as potentially "an extinction event" for the global ecosystem of China-focused nonprofits.

---

### Editorial standards

**Author attribution:** Named on all publications. *The Strategist* (ASPI's commentary platform) maintains an author page. Opinions on The Strategist carry an explicit disclaimer: *"Opinions in The Strategist are the authors', not ASPI's."*

**Corrections policy:** The Strategist has a stated corrections policy: *"The Strategist promptly corrects any factual error, appending a correction notice to the article."* This is publicly stated; its application has been tested (see track record below).

**Peer review:** ASPI describes its research reports as undergoing "rigorous internal and external peer review." In practice, this means named external reviewers from invited academics — not double-blind peer review submitted to independent journals. Quality appears to vary by publication. *The Strategist* is an opinion/analysis platform with no peer review. The flagship 2020 "Uyghurs for Sale" report listed two named reviewers (one a then-postdoctoral researcher) and unnamed others.

**No external press council or editorial standards body** audits ASPI's publications.

---

### Track record

**Documented formal apology (2020):** ASPI's report "Hunting the Phoenix" (author: Alex Joske) falsely connected Professor Wenlong Cheng of Monash University to the Thousand Talents Plan and China's defence industry. ASPI issued a formal written apology withdrawing the claim. Downstream consequence: the Australian Research Council used the report for a list of 32 academics; 30 of 32 were subsequently cleared of national security concerns.

**Disputed report not retracted (2018):** ASPI's International Cyber Policy Centre published a report on Australia's digital identity program. The Digital Transformation Agency publicly stated the report "was inaccurate and contained many factual errors," including an unfounded comparison to China's social credit system, noting that written corrections provided pre-publication were not addressed. ASPI did not retract.

**Varghese Review finding on editorial conduct (2024):** Former DFAT Secretary Peter Varghese's independent review — the most authoritative external assessment of ASPI — found that then-Executive Director Peter Jennings had engaged in "op-ed overreach," publishing articles "based on personal opinions rather than deep ASPI research" and containing "partisan commentary and even personal criticism." The government accepted the review's core findings.

**Defence expenditure methodology dispute (2025):** Defence Industry Minister Pat Conroy publicly alleged ASPI changed its methodology for calculating defence spending after the 2022 election in a manner that disadvantaged Labor's figures. ASPI rejected the allegation. Not independently resolved.

**Uyghurs for Sale (2020) — contested, not retracted:** The report claiming a systematic Uyghur forced labour transfer program received wide international coverage and influenced corporate supply-chain audits. It has not been retracted. A legal analysis published via Pearls and Irritations (Jaqueline James) identified methodological issues, citation timing anomalies, and alleged evidentiary weaknesses. The critique has not been reviewed in a peer-reviewed forum, and the report's core claims have been partially corroborated by subsequent corporate audits and independent researchers.

**Documented strengths:**
- **Critical Technology Tracker** (74 technology categories, 9M+ research papers over 21 years): large-scale, data-driven, cited by Western governments
- **Disinformation and influence operations research**: Twitter removed 2,160 Chinese state-linked accounts in December 2021 as a direct result of ASPI analysis
- **Xinjiang Data Project satellite imagery**: methodology characterised as sound by independent observers; Chinese rebuttals have focused on relabelling facilities, not disputing the imagery

---

### Declared mission and known biases

**Stated mission:** ASPI was created to provide "contestability" and "alternative sources of advice" and to "contribute an informed and independent voice." Its founding documents stressed that ASPI's credibility would "substantially be determined by the reality and appearance of independence."

**Output pattern and critical assessment:** ASPI's hawkish, pro-AUKUS, pro-Western orientation is widely acknowledged — including in the Varghese Review. Former ASPI founding director Hugh White (ANU) has argued ASPI has abandoned its founding principle of "no ASPI view" and now takes a single institutional position on China aligned with "Canberra orthodoxy." Former Foreign Minister Bob Carr describes it as providing "a one-sided, pro-American view of the world." Former PM Paul Keating has called it "a pro-US cell."

The Varghese Review makes the most operationally useful distinction: ASPI's *research output* has produced "some groundbreaking analysis"; its *Strategist op-eds* have crossed into partisanship.

Critics from Chinese state media (Xinhua, People's Daily, Global Times) characterise ASPI as a "disinformation factory." These sources must be heavily discounted — they represent official output from the primary subject of ASPI's critical research.

**Selection and framing bias:** ASPI's research focus is concentrated on China as a strategic threat, US alliance relationships, and defence capability. Coverage of topics where Western governments bear responsibility (e.g., civilian harm from Western weapons, domestic surveillance) is sparse.

### Appropriate use

**Reliable for:**
- **Data-driven research projects** (Critical Technology Tracker, Xinjiang Data Project satellite analysis, influence operations attribution): methodologically more defensible, the work that has withstood scrutiny
- **Defence capability and budget analysis**: historically respected even by critics
- **Framing of mainstream Australian strategic policy consensus**: ASPI reflects the position of Australia's security establishment, which is useful context even if it's not independent of it

**Use with caution:**
- **China-related analysis and reporting**: the US State Department funded ~70% of this work; that is a material conflict requiring disclosure in any citation. With 2025 funding frozen, the institutional position going forward is uncertain
- **Strategist op-eds**: explicitly opinion; the Varghese Review found some crossed into partisanship; treat as commentary, not research

**Not reliable for:**
- A neutral view of China–Australia relations, US alliance debates, or AUKUS — ASPI has a declared institutional stake in these questions
- Independent assessment of Australian government policy — despite the "independence" framing, ASPI is government-established, majority government-funded, and the Varghese Review found the independence was compromised in practice

**Disclosure requirement when citing:** Any citation of ASPI's China-related research should disclose the US State Department funding relationship.
