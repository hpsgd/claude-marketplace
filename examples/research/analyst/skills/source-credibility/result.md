## Source credibility: Australian Strategic Policy Institute (ASPI)

**Date of assessment:** 2026-04-30
**Source type:** Think tank / policy research organisation

### Ownership and funding

ASPI is an independent, non-partisan think tank established by the Australian Government in 2001. It is incorporated as a company limited by guarantee and governed by a council of directors.

**Funding sources (as disclosed in ASPI annual reports and on its website):**

| Funder category | Examples | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Australian Government (founding) | Department of Defence, DFAT, Home Affairs | Largest single funding bloc historically |
| Foreign governments | US State Department, UK Foreign Commonwealth & Development Office, European Commission | Named in annual reports |
| Defence industry | Lockheed Martin, BAE Systems, Northrop Grumman, Thales, Boeing | Named in annual reports |
| Other corporate | Technology companies, telecommunications | Smaller share |
| Grants / competitive | Various | Varies by year |

Disclosure source: ASPI publishes a funding disclosure page at aspi.org.au and releases annual reports naming funders by category. This is above-average transparency for an Australian think tank. However, per-project funding attribution is not disclosed — it is not always possible to determine which donor funded which specific report.

**Bias implication:** The defence-industry and government-defence funding relationship is a flag for defence-procurement research specifically. It does not mean individual claims are fabricated — but conclusions favouring defence investment warrant cross-checking, particularly where donor companies stand to benefit. Non-defence ASPI research (e.g. Pacific development, health security) carries less commercial conflict.

### Editorial standards

- **Corrections policy:** ASPI does not publish a formal corrections policy. Errors identified after publication are typically addressed through updated web versions with a notation, but there is no systematic public corrections log.
- **Peer review:** ASPI reports are not peer-reviewed in the academic sense. Significant reports go through internal editorial review and sometimes external expert comment — below the standard of a refereed journal.
- **Named accountable authors:** Yes. All ASPI publications carry named authors with stated credentials and current roles. Authors are identifiable and contactable.
- **Press council / regulatory membership:** ASPI is not a media organisation and is not a member of the Australian Press Council. No external editorial accountability mechanism exists.
- **Source attribution:** ASPI reports generally cite sources — government documents, satellite imagery, public databases. Some analysis relies on commercial intelligence sources whose methodology is not fully disclosed.

### Track record

**Reliability strengths:**

ASPI's Xinjiang detention facilities reporting (2019-2020) used satellite imagery analysis and document review. The core findings were subsequently corroborated by leaked Chinese government documents (the "China Cables") and by independent researchers. The methodology was transparent enough for third-party verification.

ASPI's ICT supply chain analysis — on Huawei 5G network risk and TikTok data flows — was cited by Five Eyes governments and held up under independent scrutiny.

**Known controversies:**

The Xinjiang reporting drew criticism from some academics at Australian universities for overstating supply chain claims — specifically, for interpreting satellite imagery in a way consistent with detention but not conclusively demonstrating it. ASPI maintained its methodology. This was a genuine interpretive dispute, not fabrication.

ASPI's funding from the US State Department for China-focused research has been cited by critics as a conflict of interest. Chinese state media and some Western academics have alleged the China-critical framing reflects funder preferences. ASPI disputes this. The dispute is contested, not resolved.

**Pattern:** No major fabrications or retractions found. Primary research (satellite imagery, document analysis, infrastructure mapping) is generally verifiable. Policy framing and threat assessments are contestable and reflect the institute's perspective.

### Declared mission and known biases

**Stated mission:** "To contribute to the security, resilience and prosperity of Australia and the Indo-Pacific by shaping strategic and defence policy choices." (aspi.org.au)

**Output pattern:** ASPI's output is overwhelmingly focused on Australian defence capability, China strategic risk, Five Eyes cooperation, and Indo-Pacific security. This is consistent with its stated mission and its funding profile. It publishes comparatively little on diplomacy, peacebuilding, or non-military security (climate, economic resilience) except where these intersect with hard security.

**Bias dimensions identified:**

- **Selection bias:** Consistently prioritises defence and strategic competition framings. Topics where military solutions are less salient receive less coverage.
- **Framing bias:** Threat assessments tend toward higher-end risk estimates. Conciliatory framings of China's strategic intentions are underrepresented.
- **Source bias:** Routinely quotes defence officials, intelligence community alumni, and industry. Critics of Australian defence posture are less represented.
- **Commercial bias:** Defence-industry donor relationship warrants scrutiny on procurement-specific reporting. ASPI advocates for capability investments (submarines, strike aircraft, cyber) that benefit those donors.

**Critical distinction:** These are bias patterns — systematic tendencies in framing and coverage selection. They are not the same as errors. ASPI can have predictable framing and still report individual facts accurately. Both dimensions matter; they are different problems.

**Overall credibility:** Medium-High

### Appropriate use

**ASPI is reliable for:**

- Primary research on Chinese military and cyber capability (infrastructure mapping, satellite analysis, document review)
- ICT supply chain analysis — Huawei, semiconductor dependencies, undersea cable networks
- Factual summaries of Australian defence procurement decisions and capability gaps
- Quantitative data on defence spending, order of battle, force structure comparisons

**ASPI should not be used as the sole source for:**

- Policy recommendations on Australia-China diplomatic relations — framing is consistently hardline; balance against sources with different orientations (e.g. Lowy Institute)
- Defence procurement cost-benefit analysis — commercial funder relationships create a conflict of interest on capability-investment questions
- Claims about intent (as opposed to capability) — ASPI's intent assessments tend toward the more threatening interpretation; these are analytical judgements, not established facts

**Cross-check against:** Lowy Institute (more varied perspectives), International Crisis Group (diplomatic framing), academic researchers critical of ASPI methodology (Australian Journal of International Affairs, Journal of Indo-Pacific Affairs), and primary government sources where available.

**Journalist note for this scenario:** For a piece on defence procurement, ASPI is a useful and credible source for capability and spending data. Describe it as a think tank with known defence-sector funding. Do not cite it as an independent or neutral authority on procurement decisions that benefit its donors. Disclosing the funding context in the article is good practice.
