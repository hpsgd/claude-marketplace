## Research: Mandatory climate risk disclosure for listed companies — OECD overview

**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Tier:** Standard
**Sources:** 7

### What has been legislated

Several OECD jurisdictions have moved from voluntary TCFD-aligned guidance to enforceable statutory requirements in the past two years.

**European Union — CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive)**
The CSRD entered force in January 2024. It applies to approximately 50,000 companies — large EU-listed companies, large unlisted companies meeting two of three size thresholds (balance sheet €20m+, turnover €40m+, 250+ employees), and non-EU companies with EU-listed securities generating €150m+ EU turnover. Reporting is against the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), which adopt **double materiality**: companies must disclose both how climate risks affect the company (financial materiality) and how the company affects the climate (impact materiality). Third-party assurance is required. Source: EU Commission Official Journal, Directive 2022/2464.

**United Kingdom — TCFD-aligned mandatory disclosure**
The FCA made TCFD-aligned climate-related disclosure mandatory for premium-listed companies from financial year 2021, extended to a broader set of listed companies and large private companies in subsequent years. Reporting follows TCFD's four pillars: governance, strategy, risk management, metrics and targets. The FCA has signalled alignment with IFRS S2 but does not adopt ISSB standards directly. Source: FCA Policy Statement PS21/23.

**Australia — AASB S2 (Treasury Laws Amendment Act 2024)**
Australia legislated mandatory climate-related financial disclosures in September 2024. Obligations phase in by entity size:

- Group 1 (NFPS > $500m or assets > $1bn): FY2025
- Group 2 (NFPS > $50m or assets > $250m): FY2026
- Group 3 (smaller entities, superannuation funds): FY2027–FY2028

AASB S2 is substantially aligned with ISSB IFRS S2. ASIC is the enforcement body. Source: ASIC regulatory guidance; Treasury Laws Amendment (Financial Market Infrastructure and Other Measures) Act 2024.

**New Zealand — XRB Climate-Related Disclosures**
NZ enacted mandatory disclosure under the Financial Sector (Climate-related Disclosures and Other Matters) Amendment Act 2021 — among the first statutory mandates globally. Reporting began for FY2022 for climate reporting entities (large listed issuers, large banks, large insurers, large investment managers). The framework is TCFD-aligned and was developed by the External Reporting Board (XRB). Source: XRB Aotearoa NZ Climate Standards NZ CS 1.

**Japan — TCFD-aligned mandatory disclosure**
Japan's FSA introduced mandatory TCFD-aligned climate disclosure for prime market-listed companies via Cabinet Office Ordinance revision in 2023. Coverage: governance, strategy, risk, metrics and targets. Assurance is not yet mandatory. Source: FSA Cabinet Office Ordinance, June 2023.

### What remains voluntary or proposed

**United States — SEC Final Rule (stayed)**
The SEC adopted a final climate-disclosure rule in March 2024 requiring registered companies to disclose material climate-related risks, Scope 1 and 2 emissions (larger filers), and financial statement effects. The rule was immediately challenged in multiple federal circuits and the SEC voluntarily stayed implementation pending litigation. As of April 2026, the rule's future is uncertain following changes in SEC leadership. Many large US companies continue voluntary TCFD or ISSB-aligned reporting. Source: SEC Final Rule 33-11275; SEC stay announcement March 2024.

**Canada — proposed, not finalised**
Canada has no mandatory climate disclosure legislation. The Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA) published a proposed rule in 2021 (National Instrument 51-107) that has not been finalised. Large Canadian companies report voluntarily against TCFD. Source: CSA Staff Notice 51-362; CSA consultation update 2023.

**Other OECD members**
Switzerland and South Korea have introduced varying requirements for large listed companies, most TCFD-aligned. Several other OECD members rely on the EU CSRD cascade for obligations applying to EU subsidiaries, rather than domestic legislation.

### Where significant variation exists

Variation across jurisdictions is substantial across four dimensions:

**Company scope.** EU CSRD covers ~50,000 companies including large private companies and foreign issuers meeting EU turnover thresholds. Australian AASB S2 covers large listed and unlisted entities above size thresholds. The UK focuses on listed companies. NZ focuses on financial-sector entities. Japan covers prime-market listed companies only.

**Materiality framework.** This is the most significant conceptual split. EU CSRD requires double materiality (impact + financial). ISSB IFRS S2, adopted by Australia and aligned with by the UK, uses financial materiality only — disclosure is required where climate risks could affect the company's financial condition, not where the company affects the environment. This changes the scope of what must be analysed and disclosed.

**Assurance requirements.** EU CSRD requires limited assurance from FY2024, escalating to reasonable assurance. Australia has signalled phased assurance but has not finalised requirements. The UK, NZ, and Japan do not currently mandate assurance.

**Scope 3 emissions.** EU CSRD requires Scope 3. ISSB IFRS S2 (and Australian AASB S2) requires Scope 3 where material, with a one-year phase-in. The SEC's proposed rule included Scope 3 for large filers but this was dropped under industry pressure in the final rule. NZ requires Scope 3 where the entity has set Scope 3 targets.

### Key uncertainties

**SEC rule litigation status.** The US rule is stayed pending federal court proceedings. It is unclear whether it will survive in its current form, be remanded for revision, or be withdrawn. This creates a significant gap in OECD coverage given the size of US capital markets.

**EU member-state transposition delays.** CSRD transposition deadlines have been missed by several member states. There is variation in implementation timetables and uncertainty about whether smaller in-scope entities will face enforcement before domestic legislation is finalised.

**ISSB / jurisdictional alignment.** ISSB published IFRS S1 and S2 in June 2023. Australia and the UK are broadly aligned; Japan is partially aligned; the EU ESRS diverges meaningfully (double materiality, additional topics). No binding international mechanism requires ISSB adoption, so "alignment" in practice ranges from near-identical (Australia) to substantially different (EU).

**AU/NZ implementation readiness.** ASIC has noted preparedness concerns for Group 2 and 3 entities. XRB has noted that many NZ climate reporting entities struggled with first-cycle requirements. Early-year enforcement leniency may be material to how binding the mandates are in practice.
