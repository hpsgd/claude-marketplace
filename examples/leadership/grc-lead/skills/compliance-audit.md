# Compliance Audit: GDPR — Rentora

## Audit Summary

- **Framework:** GDPR (EU) 2016/679
- **Scope:** Rentora property rental platform — tenant and landlord personal data, AWS eu-west-1 hosted
- **Date:** 2026-04-15
- **Auditor:** GRC Lead

## Applicable Articles

Given Rentora's data (names, addresses, ID documents, bank account details, rental history) and jurisdiction (France, Germany, Netherlands — all EU member states), the following GDPR articles are in scope:

- Art. 5 — Data processing principles (lawfulness, purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity/confidentiality)
- Art. 6 — Lawful basis for processing
- Art. 9 — Special category data (ID documents may contain health/biometric data depending on document type)
- Art. 13–14 — Transparency and privacy notices
- Art. 15–22 — Data subject rights (access, rectification, erasure, portability, restriction, objection)
- Art. 25 — Data protection by design and by default
- Art. 32 — Security of processing
- Art. 33–34 — Breach notification (72-hour to supervisory authority, notification to affected individuals)
- Art. 35 — DPIA (bank account details + ID documents at scale likely triggers this)
- Art. 44–49 — Cross-border transfers (not directly applicable — data stays in eu-west-1; verified below)

Articles not in scope for this audit: Art. 8 (children's data — no evidence of under-16 users), Art. 10 (criminal convictions data), Art. 26 (joint controller arrangements — single controller model).

## Gap Register

| Gap ID | Requirement | Severity | Description | Exposure |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| G-001 | Art. 35 — DPIA | Critical | No DPIA exists for processing ID documents and bank account details at scale. Processing is currently unlawful — Art. 35 DPIA is mandatory before this processing can proceed | Regulatory action; processing may need to stop until DPIA is completed and approved |
| G-002 | Art. 6 — Lawful basis | Critical | No documented lawful basis for any processing activity. Without documented basis, every processing activity is presumptively unlawful | Fine up to €20M or 4% global turnover; potential enforcement notice requiring processing to cease |
| G-003 | Art. 13 — Privacy notice | High | No privacy notice found. Data subjects (tenants, landlords) must be informed at point of collection about the processing of their data, including bank account details and ID documents | Regulatory fine; subject complaints to CNIL, BfDI, or Dutch AP |
| G-004 | Art. 5(1)(f) / Art. 32 — S3 encryption | High | S3 buckets not configured with server-side encryption. ID documents and potentially bank account details stored unencrypted | Data breach risk; Art. 32 security obligation not met; fine exposure |
| G-005 | Art. 33 — Breach notification process | High | No breach notification runbook or process found. 72-hour notification window to supervisory authority is a hard legal obligation | If a breach occurs, missing the 72h window is an automatic additional violation |
| G-006 | Art. 20 — Data portability | High | No data export mechanism exists. Tenants and landlords have the right to receive their data in a machine-readable format | Individual complaints; enforcement action from supervisory authorities |
| G-007 | Art. 17 — Right to erasure | Medium | Deletion capability exists but is admin-only. Subjects cannot self-initiate erasure. Process relies on humans receiving requests and manually executing | Slow SAR response times; failure to meet 30-day deadline creates violation risk |
| G-008 | Art. 5(1)(e) — Storage limitation | Medium | No automated data retention/deletion policy. Manual-only cleanup. Rental history and ID documents retained indefinitely by default | Data stored beyond necessity; potential Art. 5 violation |
| G-009 | Art. 32 — Audit logging | Medium | Only request-level logging. No field-level logging for access to bank account details, ID documents, or rental history | Inability to detect unauthorised access; Art. 32 security obligation partially unmet |
